Indirect Allorecognition

  1. Acquisition: Recipient APCs ingest donor debris.
  2. Processing & Presentation: Peptides loaded on self‑MHC II.
  3. Recognition: Host CD4 T cells see donor peptide in self groove → help, cytokines, chronic rejection pressure.

FH vs FCS (Core Differences)

  • FH: AD, LDLR mutation → LDL↑ → tendon xanthomas, premature CAD.
  • FCS: AR, LPL deficiency → chylomicrons↑ (TG↑↑) → pancreatitis risk, not premature CAD.

Acquisition (Ingestion)

Recipient APCs patrol near the graft and ingest shed donor debris (HLA fragments and other proteins).

Processing & Presentation

APCs process donor proteins into peptides and load them onto self MHC II for CD4 T‑cell surveillance.

Recognition & Activation

Host CD4 T cells recognize foreign peptide in self groove → provide help (cytokines, B‑cell support) → chronic rejection pressure.

    FH vs FCS — Animated Differences

    Ready — Step 1: Normal LDL–LDLR clearance (FH disrupts this).

    Why “Option C” is the key difference

    Correct: “FH is an autosomal dominant disorder caused by mutations in the LDLR gene, leading to elevated LDL.”

    FH (Familial Hypercholesterolemia)

    • Inheritance: Autosomal dominant (classically).
    • Gene/Defect: LDLR mutation → ↓LDL uptake.
    • Lipid: LDL ↑↑ (often > 190 mg/dL; homozygous much higher).
    • Clinical: Tendon xanthomas, premature CAD.

    FCS (Familial Chylomicronemia Syndrome)

    • Inheritance: Autosomal recessive.
    • Gene/Defect: LPL deficiency → chylomicrons accumulate.
    • Lipid: TG ↑↑↑ (chylomicrons, pancreatitis risk).
    • Clinical: No premature CAD (chylomicrons relatively non‑atherogenic in FCS).
    Why the other options are wrong
    • A: LPL deficiency describes FCS, not FH.
    • B: Strict dietary fat restriction is cornerstone for FCS, not sufficient for FH (often needs statins/PCSK9i).
    • D: Tendon xanthomas + premature CAD are FH features, not FCS.
