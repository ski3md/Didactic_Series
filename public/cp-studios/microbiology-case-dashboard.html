Case-Based Diagnostic Algorithms

Case 1: Skin Abscess

A 58-year-old patient with poorly controlled diabetes presents with a large, painful, fluctuant abscess on his thigh. Pus is aspirated and sent for Gram stain and culture.

Gram Stain Result: Numerous neutrophils and Gram-positive cocci in clusters are seen.

What is the next best test to perform on the isolate?

Catalase Test Result: Positive (vigorous bubbling is observed).

Interpretation: The organism is a *Staphylococcus* species. Now we must differentiate the most virulent species from the others.

Tube Coagulase Test Result: Positive (a fibrin clot is formed after 4 hours of incubation).

The organism is identified as *Staphylococcus aureus*. What is the final critical step?

Susceptibility Result: A cefoxitin disk screen is performed. The zone of inhibition is <21 mm, indicating resistance. Subsequent MIC testing confirms oxacillin resistance.

Mechanism: This indicates the presence of the `mecA` gene, which codes for an altered penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a).

Final Diagnosis: Methicillin-Resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA)

Clinical Correlation: MRSA is a primary cause of purulent skin and soft tissue infections. The presence of the `mecA` gene confers resistance to all β-lactam antibiotics. The appropriate treatment is vancomycin or other agents active against MRSA.

Case 2: Bloody Diarrhea

A 7-year-old child presents to the emergency department with a 2-day history of severe abdominal cramps and bloody diarrhea. The family reports attending a picnic where the child ate an undercooked hamburger. A stool culture is ordered.

Culture Results: The stool is plated on MacConkey (MAC) and Sorbitol-MacConkey (SMAC) agar.

MAC plate: Shows abundant pink colonies.

SMAC plate: Shows abundant colorless colonies.

What is the significance of the SMAC plate result?

Interpretation: The pink colonies on MAC indicate a lactose-fermenting organism, consistent with *E. coli*. The colorless colonies on SMAC indicate the isolate does **not** ferment sorbitol. This phenotype is highly characteristic of *E. coli* serotype O157:H7.

What is the essential next step to confirm the diagnosis and guide clinical management?

Shiga Toxin Assay Result: A direct EIA for Shiga toxins 1 and 2 from the stool culture broth is POSITIVE.

⚠️ Clinical Trap: Antibiotic treatment is contraindicated for STEC infections. It can induce the SOS response in the bacteria, leading to increased Shiga toxin production and a higher risk of developing Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS).

Final Diagnosis: Shiga toxin-producing *E. coli* (STEC)

Clinical Correlation: This infection is a common cause of foodborne outbreaks. The primary virulence mechanism is the production of Shiga toxins, which can lead to hemorrhagic colitis and HUS (triad of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, and acute kidney injury). Management is supportive care only.

Stains, AST & Key Biochemical Tests
Gram Stain Method Explained

The Gram stain is a **differential stain** that separates bacteria into two groups (Gram-positive and Gram-negative) based on their cell wall structure, guiding initial antibiotic choice.

Gram-Positive Cell Wall

Features a very **thick, multi-layered peptidoglycan mesh** that traps the primary stain.

[Image of Gram-positive bacterial cell wall structure]

Gram-Negative Cell Wall

Features a **thin peptidoglycan layer** and an **outer membrane** that is easily disrupted by decolorizer.

[Image of Gram-negative bacterial cell wall structure]

Procedure & Interpretation

  1. Primary Stain (Crystal Violet): All bacteria are stained purple.
  2. Mordant (Gram's Iodine): Forms a large, insoluble Crystal Violet-Iodine (CV-I) complex inside cells. All cells remain purple.
  3. Decolorizer (Acetone-Alcohol): The critical differential step.
    • Gram-Negative: Outer membrane dissolves, thin wall allows CV-I complex to escape. Cells become colorless.
    • Gram-Positive: Thick peptidoglycan dehydrates and shrinks, trapping the CV-I complex. Cells remain purple.
  4. Counterstain (Safranin): Stains the colorless Gram-negative bacteria pink/red. Gram-positive bacteria remain purple.

Final Result: Gram-Positive = **Purple**. Gram-Negative = **Pink/Red**.

MCQ Exam Focus: A question will describe a clinical scenario and ask for the "most likely finding on Gram stain." You must link the disease (e.g., meningitis in a newborn) to the organism (*S. agalactiae*) and its Gram stain morphology (Gram-positive cocci in chains).

Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing (AST)

Clinical Breakpoints

Breakpoints are established MIC values used to categorize an organism as Susceptible (S), Intermediate/Susceptible-Dose Dependent (I/SDD), or Resistant (R). They are determined by organizations like CLSI and are based on pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) data, clinical outcome data, and microbiological population distributions. They are specific for each bug-drug combination.

Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC)

Definition: The lowest concentration of an antimicrobial that inhibits visible growth of a microorganism in vitro.

Interpretation: The MIC is compared against clinical breakpoints to determine the final category (S, I, or R).

Mueller-Hinton (MH) Agar

MH agar is the standardized medium for Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion and gradient diffusion (E-test) due to its specific properties allowing for predictable antibiotic diffusion.

Kirby-Bauer Disk Diffusion

Antibiotic disks are placed on an inoculated MH plate. The **zone of inhibition** diameter is measured to determine S, I, or R.

E-test (Gradient Diffusion)

A strip with an antibiotic gradient is placed on an MH plate. The MIC is read where the zone of inhibition intersects the strip.

High-Yield Biochemical Assays

Tests for Gram-Positive Cocci

Catalase Test

Differentiates: Staph (+) from Strep (-).
(+) Result: Bubbles (H₂O₂ → O₂).

Coagulase Test

Identifies: *S. aureus* (+) from CoNS (-).
(+) Result: Fibrin clot formation.

CAMP Test

Identifies: *S. agalactiae* (GBS). Detects CAMP factor, which enhances hemolysis by *S. aureus*.
(+) Result: Arrowhead of enhanced hemolysis.

Bacitracin (A-disk) Susceptibility

Identifies: *S. pyogenes* (GAS) is Susceptible.
(+) Result: Any zone of inhibition.

Optochin (P-disk) Susceptibility

Identifies: *S. pneumoniae* is Susceptible.
(+) Result: Zone of inhibition ≥14 mm.

Bile Solubility

Confirms: *S. pneumoniae*. Bile salts activate autolysins that lyse the cell wall.
(+) Result: Colony disappears/dissolves.

PYR Test

Identifies: *Enterococcus* spp. and *S. pyogenes*. Detects pyrrolidonyl arylamidase.
(+) Result: Red color change.

Hippurate Hydrolysis

Identifies: *S. agalactiae* and *Listeria*.
(+) Result: Deep purple color.

Penicillin Susceptibility in *S. pneumoniae*

Due to widespread resistance via altered PBPs, penicillin MICs are crucial. An **oxacillin disk screen** is used first; if the zone is <20 mm, it suggests resistance, and a full penicillin MIC must be performed. Breakpoints for penicillin vary by site of infection (meningitis vs. non-meningitis).

Key Tests for Gram-Negative Rods

Oxidase Test

Differentiates: *Pseudomonas*, *Neisseria* (+) from Enterobacterales (-).
(+) Result: Purple color.

Motility Test

Differentiates: *E. coli, Proteus (+)* from *Klebsiella, Shigella (-)*.
(+) Result: Turbidity away from stab line in semi-solid agar.

H₂S Production

Differentiates: *Salmonella, Proteus (+)* from *Shigella, E. coli (-)*.
(+) Result: Black precipitate in TSI or HE agar.

Voges-Proskauer (VP)

Differentiates: *Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Serratia (+)* from *E. coli (-)*.
(+) Result: Red color after adding reagents.

Nitrate to Nitrite Conversion

Characteristic of: Most Enterobacterales. Key component of urine dipstick test for UTI.
(+) Result: Red color after adding reagents A & B.

Gram-Positive Bacteria

Vancomycin Resistance (`vanA`, `vanB`) in *Enterococcus*

Mechanism: Vancomycin normally binds to the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors, blocking cell wall synthesis. Resistance genes alter this target.

`vanA` Gene Cluster: Induces high-level resistance to both vancomycin and teicoplanin by changing the target to **D-Ala-D-Lac**. This is the most common and clinically significant type.

`vanB` Gene Cluster: Induces variable resistance to vancomycin only (remains susceptible to teicoplanin) by also changing the target to **D-Ala-D-Lac**.

Bacillus anthracis

Lab ID: Large, Gram-positive rod, spore-forming. Non-motile, non-hemolytic. Colonies are dry, tenacious, with a "medusa head" appearance.

Clinical: Cutaneous anthrax: Painless papule evolves into a black eschar with surrounding edema. Inhalational anthrax: Flu-like symptoms rapidly progress to hemorrhagic mediastinitis (widened mediastinum on CXR) and shock.

MCQ Exam Focus: A farmer or bioterrorism scenario. Key descriptors are "non-motile, non-hemolytic large GPR" and "widened mediastinum." The differentiator from *B. cereus* is motility and hemolysis.

Corynebacterium diphtheriae

Lab ID: Pleomorphic, club-shaped GPR. Grows on Tinsdale agar (black colonies with brown halos). Metachromatic granules (Babes-Ernst bodies) seen with methylene blue stain.

Clinical: Respiratory diphtheria: Pharyngitis with a thick, gray, adherent pseudomembrane that bleeds if dislodged. Systemic effects: Toxin-mediated myocarditis and neuropathy.

MCQ Exam Focus: An unvaccinated patient with a sore throat and "gray pseudomembrane." The question will ask for the mechanism of virulence (inhibition of EF-2) or the confirmatory diagnostic test (Elek test for toxin).

Nocardia vs. Rhodococcus

Nocardia spp.: Beaded, branching, filamentous GPR. **Partially acid-fast**. Causes pulmonary, cutaneous, and brain abscesses in immunocompromised hosts.

Rhodococcus equi: Pleomorphic GPR/coccobacillus. Can have salmon-pink colonies. **Partially acid-fast**. Causes cavitary pneumonia in severely immunocompromised patients (esp. AIDS) with exposure to farm animals/manure.

MCQ Exam Focus: A vignette about an immunocompromised patient with cavitary pneumonia. A Gram stain shows "branching Gram-positive rods." The key differentiator is the Acid-Fast stain result. If it's "partially acid-fast," the answer is *Nocardia*.

Gram-Negative Bacteria

Carbapenemases (e.g., KPC)

Mechanism: Carbapenemases are β-lactamase enzymes that hydrolyze not only penicillins and cephalosporins but also carbapenems. KPC (*Klebsiella pneumoniae* Carbapenemase) is the most common in the US, encoded on a mobile plasmid.

Detection: Phenotypic tests like Carba NP or mCIM, confirmed by molecular PCR for genes like `blaKPC`.

Differential Diagnosis: Yersinia vs. Shigella

Both are non-lactose fermenting, oxidase-negative, H₂S-negative GNRs that cause GI illness, but key features differentiate them.

Shigella spp.
  • Transmission: Human-to-human (fecal-oral). Very low infectious dose.
  • Clinical: Bacillary dysentery (bloody diarrhea). *S. dysenteriae* can cause HUS.
  • Key Labs: **Non-motile**. **Urease negative**.
Yersinia enterocolitica
  • Transmission: Zoonotic (undercooked pork, contaminated water).
  • Clinical: Enterocolitis, mesenteric adenitis mimicking appendicitis.
  • Key Labs: **Urease positive**. **Motile at 25°C, non-motile at 37°C**. Grows on CIN agar ("bull's-eye" colonies).

MCQ Exam Focus: A patient with pseudoappendicitis after eating pork. The question will ask for a key lab finding. The answer will be "motile at 25°C" or "growth on CIN agar." For *Shigella*, the vignette will be bloody diarrhea in a daycare setting, and the key lab finding is "non-motile."

Non-Fermenting Gram-Negative Rods

*Pseudomonas aeruginosa*: Metallic sheen, grape-like odor, often produces blue-green pyocyanin pigment.

*Acinetobacter baumannii*: Non-motile, oxidase-negative coccobacilli. Often highly multidrug-resistant.

*Burkholderia cepacia* complex: Oxidase-positive. A major threat to CF patients.

*Stenotrophomonas maltophilia*: Oxidase-negative. Intrinsically resistant to carbapenems.

MCQ Exam Focus: A cystic fibrosis patient with pneumonia grows a GNR. The key test to differentiate *Pseudomonas* from *Burkholderia* is that both are Oxidase-positive, but *Burkholderia* is often more resistant. The key differentiator for *Stenotrophomonas* is being Oxidase-negative.

Anaerobic Bacteria

Gram-Positive Anaerobes

*Clostridium difficile*: Toxin-mediated pseudomembranous colitis.

*Actinomyces israelii*: Branching rod. Causes "lumpy jaw" infections with sulfur granules.

MCQ Exam Focus: *Actinomyces* will be described in a patient with a jaw abscess after dental work. A slide will show "sulfur granules" with radiating filaments. The key is that it's an ANAEROBE and NOT acid-fast, differentiating it from *Nocardia*.

Gram-Negative Anaerobes

*Bacteroides fragilis* group: Most common gut anaerobe. Grows on Bile Esculin Agar (BEA), turning the medium black.

*Fusobacterium necrophorum*: Causes Lemierre's syndrome (septic thrombophlebitis of the internal jugular vein). Associated with chorioamnionitis.

MCQ Exam Focus: A young adult with a severe sore throat develops neck swelling and signs of sepsis. The question will ask for the most likely organism. The answer is *Fusobacterium necrophorum*.

Spirochetes & Atypical Bacteria

Spirochetes

*Treponema pallidum* (Syphilis): Visualized by darkfield microscopy. Diagnosis is primarily by serology (RPR/VDRL screen, FTA-ABS confirm). Congenital syphilis can cause necrotizing funisitis.

*Borrelia burgdorferi* (Lyme Disease): Transmitted by *Ixodes* ticks. Diagnosed by two-tiered serology.

Obligate Intracellular & Atypical Bacteria

*Chlamydia trachomatis*: Serovars A-C (trachoma), D-K (urogenital), L1-L3 (LGV). Diagnosed by NAAT.

*Rickettsia rickettsii*: Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever. Tick-borne. Causes vasculitis.

*Mycoplasma pneumoniae*: Atypical "walking" pneumonia. No cell wall (resistant to penicillins).

MCQ Exam Focus: The classic triad for RMSF is fever, headache, and a rash that starts on the wrists/ankles and moves centrally. For *Mycoplasma*, the vignette is a young adult in a dormitory with a persistent cough and a chest x-ray that "looks worse than the patient."

Comprehensive Guide to Mycobacteriology

Specimen Collection & Processing

Sputum: 3 early morning specimens. Must be decontaminated/digested (e.g., with N-acetyl-L-cysteine and NaOH) to kill normal flora and liquefy mucus.

Direct Examination

Fluorochrome Stain (Auramine-Rhodamine): Most sensitive screening method. Bright yellow-orange rods.

Acid-Fast Stain (Kinyoun or Ziehl-Neelsen): Confirmatory. Red/fuchsia rods.

Culture & Identification

Culture: Gold standard. Liquid broth (e.g., MGIT, faster) and solid media (e.g., Lowenstein-Jensen, slower).

Nontuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM) - Runyon Groups

Based on growth rate and pigment production.

  • Group I (Photochromogens): Pigment with light. *M. kansasii*.
  • Group II (Scotochromogens): Pigment in dark. *M. gordonae*.
  • Group III (Nonphotochromogens): No pigment. *M. avium* complex (MAC).
  • Group IV (Rapid Growers): Grow in <7 days. *M. abscessus*.

MCQ Exam Focus: A question will describe an AFB culture that grew in 5 days. This points to a Rapid Grower (Group IV). Another question might describe a yellow-pigmented colony that only developed color after being moved to a lighted area, which defines a Photochromogen (Group I).

Remember that "naked" (non-enveloped) viruses are generally hardier and can be transmitted via the fecal-oral route (e.g., Picornaviruses - Polio, HAV; Reoviruses - Rotavirus). High-Yield Viruses & Histologic Findings

Cytomegalovirus (CMV)

Clinical: Mononucleosis-like illness (heterophile negative). Severe disease in immunocompromised.

Histology: Enlarged cells with "Owl's eye" intranuclear inclusions.

MCQ Exam Focus: A transplant patient with odynophagia. An esophageal biopsy shows the classic "owl's eye" inclusion. The answer is CMV.

Rabies Virus

Family: Rhabdoviridae (-ssRNA, enveloped).

Clinical: Zoonosis (bats, raccoons). Encephalitis with hydrophobia, hypersalivation. Fatal once symptoms appear.

Histology: Eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions called **Negri bodies** in Purkinje cells of the cerebellum are pathognomonic.

MCQ Exam Focus: A patient with bizarre behavior and hydrophobia after a bat bite. An image of a Negri body is shown. You will be asked to identify the finding or the virus.

Yellow Fever Virus

Family: Flaviviridae (+ssRNA, enveloped).

Clinical: Transmitted by *Aedes* mosquitoes. Causes hemorrhagic fever with jaundice and "black vomit."

Histology: Liver biopsy shows midzonal necrosis and apoptotic hepatocytes known as **Councilman bodies**.

MCQ Exam Focus: A traveler from South America with jaundice and fever. A liver biopsy shows eosinophilic apoptotic bodies. You must identify these as Councilman bodies and link them to Yellow Fever.

High-Yield Fungi

⚠️ Exam Trap: Dimorphic Fungi Tissue Forms. Don't mix these up!
- *Histoplasma*: Small yeasts within macrophages.
- *Blastomyces*: Large yeast with a single broad-based bud.
- *Coccidioides*: Large spherule filled with endospores.

Opportunistic Molds: Angioinvasion is Key

Aspergillus fumigatus

Microscopy: Septate hyphae with acute (45°) angle branching.

Clinical: Invasive disease in neutropenic patients.

MCQ Exam Focus: A neutropenic leukemia patient with fever and cavitary lung lesions. A biopsy shows septate hyphae branching at acute angles. The most likely organism is *Aspergillus*.

Mucor / Rhizopus spp. (Mucorales)

Microscopy: Non-septate, ribbon-like hyphae with right (90°) angle branching.

Clinical: Rhinocerebral disease in DKA patients.

MCQ Exam Focus: A patient with diabetic ketoacidosis presents with black, necrotic facial lesions. A biopsy image shows broad, non-septate hyphae. The answer is Mucorales.

Cryptococcus neoformans

Form: Encapsulated Yeast.

Diagnosis: India Ink stain of CSF shows halos. Cryptococcal Antigen (CrAg) test is most sensitive.

Clinical: Meningitis in AIDS patients.

MCQ Exam Focus: An AIDS patient with headache and fever. The question asks for the most sensitive test on CSF. The answer is Cryptococcal Antigen, which is more sensitive than India ink or culture.

Key Protozoa

Malaria (*Plasmodium* spp.) Diagnostics

Life Cycle: Mosquito (definitive host) injects sporozoites into human (intermediate host). Liver stage precedes blood stage.

Diagnosis: Thick (screening) and thin (speciation) blood smears are gold standard.
*P. falciparum* Key Findings: High parasitemia, multiple rings/RBC, appliqué forms, crescent/banana-shaped gametocytes.

MCQ Exam Focus: An image of a peripheral blood smear will be shown with multiple ring forms in an RBC or a classic banana-shaped gametocyte. You will be asked to identify the organism (*P. falciparum*).

Entamoeba histolytica

Clinical: Bloody diarrhea (dysentery), "flask-shaped" ulcers, liver abscess.

Diagnosis: Trophozoites with ingested RBCs in stool is pathognomonic.

MCQ Exam Focus: A patient with a liver abscess ("anchovy paste") and a history of travel. The key finding on a stool O&P image will be a trophoziote containing RBCs.

Naegleria fowleri

Clinical: Rapidly fatal Primary Amebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM) after swimming in warm freshwater.

Diagnosis: Motile amoeboid trophozoites seen on wet mount of CSF.

MCQ Exam Focus: Vignette of a teenager who went swimming in a lake a week ago and now has fulminant meningitis. The question will ask what is expected to be seen in the CSF, and the answer is "motile trophozoites."

Key Helminths (Worms)

⚠️ Exam Trap: *Taenia solium* (Pork Tapeworm) has TWO distinct diseases!
1. Ingesting LARVAE (cysticerci) in undercooked pork → **Taeniasis** (intestinal tapeworm). Human is definitive host.
2. Ingesting EGGS (fecal-oral) → **Cysticercosis**. Human is accidental intermediate host. Larvae encyst in brain (neurocysticercosis).

Nematodes (Roundworms)

*Enterobius vermicularis* (Pinworm): Perianal itching, especially at night. Diagnosis via **Scotch tape test** showing characteristic plano-convex eggs.

*Ascaris lumbricoides* (Giant roundworm): Can cause intestinal obstruction or pneumonitis (Loeffler's syndrome). Diagnosis via large, mamillated eggs in stool.

*Strongyloides stercoralis*: Unique autoinfection cycle can lead to hyperinfection syndrome in immunocompromised patients. Diagnosis via **rhabditiform larvae in stool**.

Trematodes (Flukes) & Cestodes (Tapeworms)

*Schistosoma* spp.: Penetrate skin in freshwater ("swimmer's itch"). Cause portal hypertension (*S. mansoni, S. japonicum*) or squamous cell carcinoma of the bladder (*S. haematobium*). Diagnosis by finding characteristic eggs in stool or urine. *S. mansoni* egg has a large lateral spine; *S. haematobium* has a terminal spine.

MCQ Exam Focus: A patient from Africa with hematuria. A urine sediment image shows a large egg with a terminal spine. The answer is *S. haematobium*.

Microbiology & Immunology

Serological Diagnosis of Infectious Disease

Principle: Serology detects host antibodies (IgM, IgG) or microbial antigens in patient serum. It's crucial for diagnosing non-culturable organisms or past infections.

Methodology: ELISA/EIA is the workhorse. A positive screen often requires confirmation by a more specific method (e.g., Western blot for HIV, FTA-ABS for Syphilis).

MCQ Exam Focus: A question will present a syphilis serology panel with a positive RPR and a negative FTA-ABS. You must recognize this as a likely biological false positive and identify associated conditions (e.g., lupus, pregnancy).

Host Response and Immune Evasion

Host Response in Histology: Neutrophils suggest pyogenic bacteria. Granulomas suggest mycobacteria or fungi. Eosinophils suggest helminthic infections.

Immune Evasion Mechanisms: Capsules (*S. pneumoniae*), Intracellular Survival (*Listeria*), Antigenic Variation (*N. gonorrhoeae*).

Microbiology & Molecular Pathology

Nucleic Acid Amplification Testing (NAAT)

Principle: NAAT (e.g., PCR) amplifies specific DNA or RNA sequences, providing highly sensitive and specific detection of microorganisms.

Applications in Micro: Primary Diagnosis (*Chlamydia*), Viral Load Monitoring (HIV), Resistance Gene Detection (`mecA`).

MCQ Exam Focus: A question asks for the best test to monitor response to HIV therapy. The answer is quantitative viral load by PCR, not CD4 count (which measures immune status) or serology (which will remain positive).

Genotypic Resistance vs. Phenotypic Susceptibility

Genotypic (Molecular): Detects a resistance gene (e.g., `mecA`). Fast, but only finds known targets.

Phenotypic (AST): Measures organism growth against the drug (e.g., MIC). Slower, but reflects the true phenotype.

Microbiology & Toxicology / Lab Management

Bacterial Toxins (Toxicology Link)

Toxin detection assays are key to diagnosis.

Examples: Shiga Toxin (*E. coli* O157:H7), Diphtheria Toxin, *C. difficile* Toxins A/B.

Biosafety Levels & Regulation (Lab Management)

BSL-3:** Organisms causing serious disease via inhalation. Requires work in a biological safety cabinet. Examples: ***M. tuberculosis*, *Brucella* spp., *Francisella tularensis*, *Coccidioides* (mold form)**.

MCQ Exam Focus: A lab tech cultures a fungus from a patient from Arizona, and it grows as a fluffy white mold. The question asks for the most important next step. The answer is to move all work into a biosafety cabinet due to the risk of aerosolizing *Coccidioides* arthroconidia.

Informatics & Quality Control

Antibiograms: An annual summary of antimicrobial susceptibility data. **Calculation:** % Susceptible = (Number of Susceptible Isolates / Total Number of Isolates Tested) x 100. Only the first isolate per patient per year is included.

Quality Control (QC): All tests require QC using ATCC reference strains to ensure accuracy.

Microbiology & Transfusion Medicine / Blood Banking / HLA

Transfusion-Transmitted Infections (TTIs) & Screening

Methodology: A dual approach of **NAT** (to shorten the window period) and **serology** is used for screening blood products.

FDA-Mandated Panel:** HIV, HCV, HBV, WNV, Zika, HTLV, Syphilis.

MCQ Exam Focus: A question will ask which TTI is most likely to be missed by serology alone in a recently infected donor. The answer will be an agent with a known window period where NAT is positive but antibodies are not yet detectable (e.g., HIV, HCV).

Bacterial Contamination of Platelets

Problem: Platelets are stored at room temperature, making them the component at highest risk for bacterial sepsis.

Common Organisms: Skin flora (*S. aureus*, CoNS).

Donor Deferral & Product Modification (HLA Link)

Leukoreduction: Reduces the risk of transmitting intracellular pathogens like **CMV** and **HTLV**. Also reduces febrile reactions and HLA alloimmunization.

Irradiation: Prevents TA-GVHD by inactivating donor T-lymphocytes.

CMV & HLA Connection: At-risk, CMV-seronegative patients (e.g., BMT recipients) require **CMV-seronegative** or **leukoreduced** blood to prevent primary CMV infection.
