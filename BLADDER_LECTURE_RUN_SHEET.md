# Bladder Lecture Run Sheet

## Session Goal

Deliver a high-yield bladder pathology lecture that gives residents a safe diagnostic scaffold for TURBT, biopsy, and cystectomy specimens.

## Recommended Length

- 30 to 40 minutes
- 10 to 12 minutes for questions and case discussion if needed

## Teaching Sequence

### 1. Why bladder matters
- Open with painless gross hematuria.
- Emphasize smoking and aromatic amine exposure.
- State the teaching contract early:
  - papillary versus flat versus invasive
  - grade
  - depth
  - variants
  - mimics

### 2. Specimen-first mindset
- Ask the room:
  - Is this TURBT, biopsy, or cystectomy?
  - Is muscularis propria present?
  - Is this papillary, flat, or invasive?
- Repeat the key line:
  - no muscularis propria, no confident exclusion of pT2

### 3. Papillary ladder
- Walk through:
  - papilloma
  - PUNLMP
  - low-grade papillary urothelial carcinoma
  - high-grade papillary urothelial carcinoma
- Teaching contrast:
  - order and polarity versus disorder and pleomorphism

### 4. Flat lesions
- Build the differential:
  - reactive atypia
  - dysplasia
  - CIS
- Use CK20, p53, and CD44 only after anchoring the H&E impression.
- Key teaching phrase:
  - stains support morphology; they do not replace morphology

### 5. Invasion and staging
- Explain lamina propria versus muscularis propria.
- Highlight TURBT pitfalls:
  - muscularis mucosae is not muscularis propria
  - tangential cuts
  - cautery artifact
- Make this the management hinge of the lecture.

### 6. Variant histology
- Cover the board-relevant set:
  - micropapillary
  - plasmacytoid
  - nested
  - sarcomatoid
  - squamous differentiation
  - adenocarcinoma/urachal patterns
- Key message:
  - some variants look deceptively bland or deceptively focal but behave aggressively

### 7. Mimics and non-neoplastic lesions
- Acute and chronic cystitis
- Hemorrhagic cystitis
- Malakoplakia
- BCG effect
- Nephrogenic adenoma
- Cystitis glandularis
- Teaching phrase:
  - context prevents overcalling

### 8. Close with a sign-out algorithm
- Papillary?
- Flat?
- Invasive?
- If invasive:
  - how deep?
  - any variant histology?
- If flat:
  - reactive versus CIS?

## High-Yield Board Pearls

- Painless gross hematuria is bladder cancer until proven otherwise.
- Smoking is the most important modifiable risk factor.
- CIS is a flat high-grade lesion with major progression risk.
- Muscularis propria invasion defines muscle-invasive disease.
- Micropapillary and plasmacytoid variants are especially dangerous.
- PUNLMP is not the same thing as low-grade carcinoma.
- Reactive atypia can have nucleoli; architecture and maturation still matter.

## Suggested Audience Questions

1. What is the single most important sentence to include in a high-grade TURBT report?
2. What separates PUNLMP from low-grade papillary urothelial carcinoma?
3. What are the common clues that a flat lesion is CIS rather than reactive?
4. Which aggressive variant would make you worry even before full staging is available?

## If You Need To Shorten The Lecture

Keep these five blocks:
1. Hematuria + risk factors
2. Papillary grading ladder
3. CIS versus reactive atypia
4. Lamina propria versus muscularis propria invasion
5. Aggressive variants
